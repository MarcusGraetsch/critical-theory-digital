# Critique of AI — Berlin Roundtable Authors

**Event:** Critical Theory in Berlin — Roundtable "Critique of AI"  
**Date:** February 2026  
**Location:** Berlin  
**Added to Project:** 2026-02-16

---

## Overview

This roundtable brought together critical theorists examining AI as a social and cultural phenomenon that cannot be separated from economic, political, and ecological conditions. Central questions:

- How do LLMs, image generators, and algorithmic systems change work and communication?
- How do they contribute to degradation of literacy, public reasoning, and shared meaning?
- How does AI shape, standardize, and feed back culture in uniform, predictable, market-driven forms?
- How might we cultivate critical reflection when the tools we use to think are making thinking nearly impossible?

## Authors

### 1. Matteo Pasquinelli

**Position:** Associate Professor in Philosophy of Science, Ca' Foscari University Venice  
**Project:** ERC project AIMODELS  
**Book:** *The Eye of the Master: A Social History of Artificial Intelligence* (Verso, 2023)  
**Award:** Deutscher Memorial Prize 2024

**Key Arguments:**
- **Labor is at the root of AI's historical development** — not just data, but human labor
- Challenges the "automation" narrative by tracing how AI systems require massive human input
- Connects AI development to histories of exploitation and colonial extraction

**Connection to Platform Power:**
- AI systems on platforms (content recommendation, moderation) hide the human labor behind them
- "Intelligent" systems are actually socio-technical assemblages of human and machine labor
- Marxist framework for understanding AI as continuation of capital-labor relations

**Essential Reading:**
- *The Eye of the Master* — social history placing labor at center of AI development
- Articles on "machinic capitalism" and AI as governance

### 2. Anna-Verena Nosthoff

**Position:** Junior Professor of Ethics of Digitalization, Carl von Ossietzky University Oldenburg  
**Affiliation:** Co-director of Critical Data Lab (HU Berlin/Uni Oldenburg)  
**Book:** *Kybernetik und Kritik: Eine Theorie digitaler Regierungskunst* (Suhrkamp, February 2026)

**Key Arguments:**
- **Kybernetisierung** (cybernetization) has captured society and even criticism itself
- Digital governance operates through feedback loops, self-regulation, and control systems
- Critique must reinvent itself to survive in cybernetic conditions
- Datafied society requires new forms of critical theory

**Connection to Platform Power:**
- Platforms operate as cybernetic systems of governance
- Self-regulation and algorithmic feedback replace democratic deliberation
- "Nudging" and behavioral modification through digital infrastructure
- Critical theory must adapt to conditions where control is distributed, not centralized

**Essential Reading:**
- *Kybernetik und Kritik* (2026) — comprehensive theory of digital governance
- "Netzwerkaffekte. Über Facebook als kybernetische Maschine und das Verschwinden des Subjekts" (2019)
- "The Obsolescence of Politics" (2019) — on Günther Anders' critique of cybernetic governance

## Synthesis: Core Themes

### 1. AI as Labor Process
All four authors emphasize that AI is not "automatic" but depends on:
- **Human labor** (Pasquinelli) — visible and invisible workers training, moderating, maintaining
- **Data extraction** (Nosthoff) — continuous harvesting of human behavior
- **Cultural flattening** (Meyer) — standardization of creativity and expression
- **Power relations** (Mülhoff) — AI as governance, not just technology

### 2. Degradation of Critical Capacities
The roundtable focused on how AI corrodes:
- **Literacy** — deep reading and writing replaced by frictionless output
- **Reasoning** — public deliberation degraded by algorithmic curation
- **Creativity** — imagination replaced by automated templates
- **Subjectivity** — the "thinking subject" dissolved into feedback loops

### 3. Platform Connection
- Platforms are primary vectors for AI deployment
- Same mechanisms: extraction, standardization, control
- AI intensifies platform power by making it appear "intelligent" and "automatic"
- Critical theory must address how AI makes platforms harder to critique (they seem "smart")
